## Problem Statement

A battery factory produces batteries whose lifetimes are independent and identically distributed with mean $\mu = 200$ hours and standard deviation $\sigma = 30$ hours (the exact distribution is unknown).

A quality inspector randomly selects $n = 100$ batteries and records their total lifetime $S_{100} = X_1 + X_2 + \cdots + X_{100}$.

**(a)** Define the standardized random variable $Z_{100}$ using the CLT. That is, express:

$$Z_{100} = \frac{S_{100} - ?}{?}$$

filling in the appropriate mean and standard deviation of $S_{100}$.

**(b)** Using the CLT approximation ($Z_{100} \approx \mathcal{N}(0,1)$), compute:

$$P(S_{100} > 20{,}500)$$

Express your answer in terms of the standard normal CDF $\Phi(\cdot)$, then approximate numerically using the values below.

**(c)** The factory guarantees a total lifetime exceeding $T$ hours for a batch of 100 batteries with probability at least 95%. Find the maximum value of $T$ (to the nearest integer).

> **Standard normal CDF reference values:**
>
> | $z$ | $\Phi(z)$ |
> |------|-----------|
> | 1.28 | 0.90 |
> | 1.645 | 0.95 |
> | 1.96 | 0.975 |
> | 2.00 | 0.977 |

**(d)** Can Chebyshev's inequality also be used to bound $P(S_{100} > 20{,}500)$? Set up the Chebyshev bound and **compare** it to the CLT approximation from part (b). Which gives a tighter result, and why?
