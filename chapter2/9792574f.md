### 3.md (Hard)

**Problem Statement: Joint Distributions, Marginalization, and Independence**

Two discrete random variables $X$ and $Y$ are defined on the same sample space. Their joint probability mass function $p_{X,Y}(x, y)$ is given by the following table:

| $p_{X,Y}(x, y)$ | $x=1$ | $x=2$ | $x=3$ |
| :--- | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| **$y=1$** | $1/10$ | $1/20$ | $1/10$ |
| **$y=2$** | $1/20$ | $2/10$ | $1/20$ |
| **$y=3$** | $1/10$ | $1/20$ | $3/10$ |

1. Determine the marginal PMFs, $p_X(x)$ and $p_Y(y)$, for all possible values of $x$ and $y$ through the process of marginalization.
2. Determine whether $X$ and $Y$ are independent random variables. Justify your answer using the definition of independence: $p_{X,Y}(x, y) = p_X(x)p_Y(y)$.
3. Calculate the conditional PMF of $X$ given that $Y = 2$, denoted as $p_{X|Y}(x|2)$.
4. Let $Z = X + Y$. Calculate the expected value $E[Z]$.
5. If a student assumes $X$ and $Y$ are independent, they might calculate $var(X+Y) = var(X) + var(Y)$. Based on your finding in part (2), explain whether this specific formula for the variance of the sum is mathematically valid for this joint distribution.
