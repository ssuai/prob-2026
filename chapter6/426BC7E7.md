Calls arrive at an emergency hotline according to a Poisson process with rate $\lambda = 3$ calls per hour. Let $N_\tau$ denote the number of arrivals in an interval of length $\tau$, with PMF:

$$P(k,\tau) = \frac{e^{-\lambda\tau}(\lambda\tau)^k}{k!}, \quad k = 0,1,2,\ldots$$

Let $Y_k$ denote the time of the $k$-th arrival (Erlang of order $k$), with PDF and moments:

$$f_{Y_k}(y) = \frac{\lambda^k y^{k-1} e^{-\lambda y}}{(k-1)!}, \quad y \geq 0, \qquad E[Y_k] = \frac{k}{\lambda}, \quad \text{var}(Y_k) = \frac{k}{\lambda^2}$$

**1.** Find $E[Y_4]$ and $\text{var}(Y_4)$.

- **(a)** $E[Y_4] =$ ________, $\quad \text{var}(Y_4) =$ ________

**2.** A supervisor steps away at $t = 1$ hour and returns at $t = 1.5$ hours. She is told that 3 calls came in while she was away. The **fresh-start property** of the Poisson process states: for any $t > 0$, letting $T$ be the time of the first arrival after $t$,

$$P(T - t > s) = P(0, s) = e^{-\lambda s}, \quad s \geq 0$$

and $T - t$ is independent of the history up to time $t$.

- **(b)** What is the expected additional time from $t = 1.5$ until the next call? Does the fact that 3 calls arrived during her absence change this answer? ________

**3.** The Erlang CDF has no closed form in general, but it can be expressed exactly using the Poisson PMF through the following identity:

$$P(Y_k \leq t) = P(N_t \geq k)$$

**(c)** Find the probability that the 4th call arrives within 1 hour, which is equivalent to the probability that 4 or more calls arrive during that 1-hour interval.
 $P(Y_4 \leq 1) =$ ________

________

---

### Answer:

**(a)** $E[Y_4] = \dfrac{4}{3}$ hours, $\quad \text{var}(Y_4) = \dfrac{4}{9}$ hours²

**(b)** $E[T - 1.5] = \dfrac{1}{3}$ hour. The 3 calls during her absence are irrelevant — the fresh-start property makes the future independent of all past history.

**(c)** $P(Y_4 \leq 1) = 1 - 13e^{-3}$

---

### Explanation:

**Step 1.** Apply Erlang mean and variance formulas with $k = 4$, $\lambda = 3$:

$$E[Y_4] = \frac{4}{3}, \qquad \text{var}(Y_4) = \frac{4}{9}$$

**Step 2.** By the fresh-start property, $T - 1.5 \sim \text{Exponential}(\lambda = 3)$ regardless of what happened before $t = 1.5$. Therefore:

$$E[T - 1.5] = \frac{1}{\lambda} = \frac{1}{3} \text{ hour}$$

The information "3 calls arrived while she was away" belongs to the history up to $t = 1.5$, which the fresh-start property renders irrelevant. The answer is $1/3$ hour unconditionally.

**Step 3.** Prove $P(Y_k \leq t) = P(N_t \geq k)$.

The event $\{Y_k \leq t\}$ means the $k$-th arrival occurs **by time $t$**. This happens if and only if at least $k$ arrivals have occurred in $[0, t]$, i.e., $\{N_t \geq k\}$. The two events are logically identical:

$$\{Y_k \leq t\} \iff \{N_t \geq k\} \quad \therefore \quad P(Y_k \leq t) = P(N_t \geq k)$$

**Step 4.** Apply this identity with $k = 4$, $t = 1$, so $N_1 \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda\tau = 3)$:

$$P(Y_4 \leq 1) = P(N_1 \geq 4) = 1 - P(N_1 \leq 3) = 1 - \sum_{k=0}^{3} P(k,\, 1)$$

**Step 5.** Compute each Poisson term with $\lambda\tau = 3$:

$$P(0,1) = e^{-3}, \qquad P(1,1) = 3e^{-3}, \qquad P(2,1) = \frac{9}{2}e^{-3}, \qquad P(3,1) = \frac{27}{6}e^{-3} = \frac{9}{2}e^{-3}$$

**Step 6.** Sum and conclude:

$$P(N_1 \leq 3) = e^{-3}\!\left(1 + 3 + \frac{9}{2} + \frac{9}{2}\right) = e^{-3}(1 + 3 + 9) = 13e^{-3}$$

$$\therefore \quad P(Y_4 \leq 1) = 1 - 13e^{-3} \quad \blacksquare$$
