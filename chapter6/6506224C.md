## Problem

A hospital emergency department tracks two types of patient arrivals:

- **Type A (trauma):** Patients arrive as a Poisson process with rate $\lambda = 3$ per hour, 24 hours a day.
- **Type B (non-urgent):** The hospital divides the day into 1-hour slots. In each slot, independently, a non-urgent patient either arrives (probability $p = 0.4$) or does not — modeled as a Bernoulli process.

The hospital's **triage nurse** assesses each arriving patient. For each patient (regardless of type), the nurse independently flags the patient as "high priority" with probability $q = 1/3$.

**(a)** Let $T_A$ be the time (in hours) until the first Type A patient is flagged as high priority. Identify the distribution of $T_A$ and compute $E[T_A]$.

> *Hint: Think about what splitting does to a Poisson process.*

**(b)** During a given 1-hour slot, let $X$ be the **total number of high-priority patients** (from both Type A and Type B arrivals). Compute $E[X]$ and $\text{var}(X)$.

> *Hint: Condition on whether a Type B patient arrives in that slot.*

**(c) [Conceptual Trap]** A colleague claims:

> *"Since Poisson splitting produces independent subprocesses, and the Bernoulli process is also independent of the Poisson process, the total high-priority count $X$ in part (b) must be a Poisson random variable."*

Is this claim correct? Carefully justify your answer by examining the distribution of $X$. If the claim is wrong, identify exactly where the reasoning breaks down.
