## Problem 3: Independent Signals and Elliptical Outage Regions (Hard)

In an advanced digital communication receiver, an incoming transmission is decomposed into two orthogonal signal metrics, $V_1$ and $V_2$, which are both treated as continuous random variables. 

Due to asymmetrical thermal noise in the environment, $V_1$ and $V_2$ are independent from one another, but they follow different Normal Distributions:
- $V_1 \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$
- $V_2 \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 9\sigma^2)$
where $\sigma > 0$ is a known environmental constant.

**Questions:**

**(a)** Construct the complete Joint Probability Density Function $f_{V_1, V_2}(v_1, v_2)$ for the two signal metrics.

**(b)** The receiver calculates the "normalized signal intensity" as a new random variable defined by: 
$$W = V_1^2 + \left(\frac{V_2}{3}\right)^2$$
By utilizing variable transformations or integrating over an appropriate geometric region in the $V_1V_2$-plane, derive the exact probability density function (PDF) of $W$, $f_W(w)$. 

**(c)** A critical system failure ("outage") occurs if the raw signal metrics fall outside the elliptical boundary defined by the equation $9V_1^2 + V_2^2 \le 36\sigma^2$. Set up the precise multi-variable integral required to calculate the probability of a system outage, and evaluate this probability in terms of $e$. 
*(Hint: Think about transforming the region into polar coordinates to simplify the integration).*
