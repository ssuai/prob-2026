### [Medium Level] Question 2: The Digital Divide (15 Points)

A city government recently launched a new self-serve kiosk for issuing public documents. Users of the kiosk are classified into two mutually exclusive demographic groups: Young and Elderly. Based on the first month's usage logs:
* 80% of the kiosk users are Young (Let this be Event $A$, so $P(A) = 0.8$).
* 20% of the kiosk users are Elderly (Let this be Event $B$, so $P(B) = 0.2$).

To begin a session, the kiosk requires a session password and a biometric authentication process.

**(2-a)** [Permutations] The kiosk generates a temporary session password consisting of an ordered arrangement of 4 distinct digits selected from a set of 6 available digits $\{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}$ without replacement. Calculate the number of possible unique session passwords that can be generated.

**(2-b)** [Independence] The biometric authentication requires two separate scans: a fingerprint scan and a facial recognition scan. A "Complete Authentication Error" (Let this be Event $E$) occurs **ONLY IF** both the fingerprint scan and the facial recognition scan fail. 
* For a Young user (Event $A$), the fingerprint scan fails with a probability of $\frac{1}{4}$ and the facial recognition fails with a probability of $\frac{1}{5}$. 
* For an Elderly user (Event $B$), the fingerprint scan fails with a probability of $\frac{1}{2}$ and the facial recognition fails with a probability of $\frac{3}{5}$. 

Given that the two biometric scans fail strictly independently of each other, calculate the conditional probabilities of a Complete Authentication Error for both groups: $P(E|A)$ and $P(E|B)$.

**(2-c)** [Bayes' Rule] The system administrator just received an alert that a "Complete Authentication Error" (Event $E$) has occurred on one of the terminals. Using Bayes' Rule and the probabilities derived in the previous step, calculate the posterior probability that the user who triggered the error was an Elderly user ($P(B|E)$).
